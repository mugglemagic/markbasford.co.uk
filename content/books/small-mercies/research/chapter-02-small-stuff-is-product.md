# Chapter 02 Research — The Small Stuff Is the Product

## Dan Saffer's Microinteractions Framework

### Definition
- Dan Saffer defines microinteractions as "tiny pieces of functionality that only do one thing."
- Examples: muting your phone, rating an app, setting a status message, toggling a switch.
- They are "the contained product moments that do one small task."

### The Four Components
1. **Trigger** — the event that starts the microinteraction. Manual triggers (user-initiated: button press, flip of a switch) or system triggers (automatic: toast done beeping, text message chime).
2. **Rules** — the constraints and logic governing the interaction (e.g., character limits on a status update).
3. **Feedback** — verification that the action was acknowledged. Can be visual, auditory, haptic, or kinetic. Example: iPhone vibrates when switched to silent.
4. **Loops & Modes** — determines duration, repetition, and whether the interaction changes over time.

## Kahneman's Peak-End Rule

### The Science
- The peak-end rule: people judge an experience based on how they felt at its most intense point (peak) and at its end, rather than the sum or average of every moment.
- First established by Daniel Kahneman et al. in 1993. Elaboration on the "snapshot model of remembered utility" (Fredrickson & Kahneman).
- Duration neglect: the length of an experience has surprisingly little effect on how it's remembered.

### The Classic Study
- Colonoscopy patients: those who underwent a longer procedure with a gentler ending rated the experience as less unpleasant than those with a shorter but abruptly-ending procedure. The longer-procedure group was also more likely to return for follow-ups.
- Implication: how an experience ends matters disproportionately.

### Meta-Analysis (2022)
- A meta-analysis published in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes* confirmed the peak-end effect across multiple domains.

## The Affect Heuristic

- People make judgments based on current emotions rather than objective analysis.
- If a microinteraction leaves someone feeling good (a satisfying toggle, a clear confirmation), that feeling colours their perception of the entire product.
- Connects to peak-end rule: microinteractions are often the peaks and ends of task flows.

---

## Bridge to the Book's Thesis

- Products are experienced as sequences of microinteractions, and memory of those products is governed by peaks and ends — not by feature counts.
- Teams obsess over features (the big stuff) and treat microinteractions as polish. But the psychology says the small stuff *is* the product, because that's what people actually remember and feel.
- This chapter establishes the scientific warrant for the whole book: microinteractions aren't decoration. They're the mechanism by which products create (or destroy) emotional memory.

## Story Candidates
1. The iPhone silent switch — one of the most beloved microinteractions ever shipped. A physical toggle with haptic feedback that tells you exactly what happened without looking.
2. Slack's loading messages — tiny moments of personality during a wait state that became part of the brand.
3. The colonoscopy study as an unexpected way into the argument — if ending matters more than duration, every "save successful" and "you're all set" is doing disproportionate emotional work.
