# Chapter 03 Research — Hello Again: Designing the Welcome Back Moment

## The Mere Exposure Effect

### Core Science
- The mere-exposure effect (also called the familiarity principle): people develop preference for things simply because they are familiar with them.
- First studied by Robert Zajonc (1968). The effect operates even without conscious recognition — subliminal exposure to stimuli still increases liking.
- Mechanism: **perceptual fluency** — repeated encounters make the brain more efficient at recognising a stimulus, and this ease of recognition produces positive feelings.

### Interpersonal Applications
- In workplaces and schools, people form stronger connections with those they encounter frequently. Even neutral or insignificant initial interactions lead to rapport through repeated exposure.
- The effect is cumulative — each encounter strengthens the positive association.

### Product Applications
- Brand recognition, loyalty programmes, and repeated customer interactions all leverage mere exposure.
- A returning user who sees familiar patterns, remembered preferences, and contextual updates is experiencing mere exposure at the interface level.

## Social Recognition and Belonging

### Why Being Recognised Matters
- Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner): people derive self-esteem from group membership and recognition within groups.
- Being greeted by name — whether by a barista, a neighbour, or a login screen — signals belonging and acknowledgement.
- Parasocial interaction research shows people form one-way emotional bonds with entities (including brands and products) that address them personally.

### The Dog-Walk Analogy
- Saying hello to someone on a dog walk is a micro-recognition event. It costs nothing, takes a second, and materially changes how both people feel about the encounter.
- The digital equivalent: "Welcome back, Mark. You left off on..." vs. a blank dashboard.

## Design Patterns for the Welcome Back Moment

### First Login vs. Returning Login
- First login: orientation, onboarding, reducing overwhelm. The user is a stranger — introduce yourself.
- Returning login: recognition, continuity, contextual updates. The user is a regular — show you remember them.
- Most products treat both identically. The ones that don't (Spotify's personalised home, Duolingo's streak reminder, GitHub's activity feed) feel noticeably warmer.

### Contextual Updates
- "Since you were last here..." patterns — showing what changed while the user was away.
- Time-aware greetings ("Good morning" vs. generic "Welcome").
- Surfacing recent activity or in-progress work.

### Anti-patterns
- Forced re-onboarding for returning users.
- Generic greetings that could apply to anyone ("Welcome to [Product]!").
- Ignoring the gap — no acknowledgement of time passed or context changed.
