# Chapter 04 Research — First Impressions in Milliseconds

## Thin-Slice Judgments — Ambady & Rosenthal

### The Original Research
- Nalini Ambady and Robert Rosenthal coined "thin slicing" in a 1992 meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin*.
- Thin slices: "short excerpts of social behavior from which perceivers can draw inferences about states, traits, and other personally-relevant characteristics."
- 1993 experiment: viewers rated teachers after 2-, 5-, and 10-second clips of nonverbal behaviour. Ratings were remarkably similar to those of people who had substantial interactions with the same teachers. Accuracy did not improve significantly between 2 and 10 seconds.
- Implication: most of the judgment happens almost immediately.

### Nonverbal Dominance
- When verbal and nonverbal cues conflict, people trust the nonverbal. Humans can control what they say more easily than facial expressions, body language, and tone.
- For interfaces: the "nonverbal" is visual design, motion, layout, spacing. Users read these before they read a single word of copy.

## The 50 Milliseconds Study — Lindgaard et al.

### The Research
- Lindgaard, Fernandes, Dudek, and Brown (2006): "Attention web designers: You have 50 milliseconds to make a good first impression!"
- Three studies tested how quickly people form opinions about web page visual appeal. Visual appeal ratings at 50ms were highly correlated with ratings at 500ms — judgments form almost instantly and are remarkably stable.
- 50ms is not enough time for analytical or reflective cognition. These are pure gut reactions driven by visual processing.

### Visual Appeal as Trust Proxy
- A British study on health websites: 94% of first impressions related to design aspects (layout, colour, imagery); only 6% addressed content.
- Visual appeal acts as a proxy for credibility: if it looks trustworthy, people assume it is trustworthy — at least long enough to engage further.
- But: aesthetics can open the door; they cannot compensate for core friction. A beautiful interface with broken flows loses trust faster than an ugly one that works.

## Confirmation Bias and First Impressions

- Once a first impression forms, subsequent information is filtered through it (confirmation bias).
- A positive 50ms impression means the user interprets ambiguous moments more charitably. A negative one means they're looking for reasons to leave.
- This is why visual design isn't vanity — it's the cognitive foundation for everything that follows.

---

## Bridge to Microinteractions

- First impressions set the stage, but they don't sustain trust. That's the job of every subsequent microinteraction.
- The handoff: Chapter 04 establishes that you have milliseconds to earn attention, and the rest of the book is about what you do with it.

## Story Candidates
1. Speed dating research — people report knowing within seconds whether there's chemistry. The rest of the date is confirmation or disconfirmation.
2. The Lindgaard study itself — 50ms is less than a blink. Show a webpage for less time than it takes to blink and people have already decided.
3. Walking into a restaurant — you've judged the lighting, the spacing, the cleanliness, the vibe before you've been handed a menu. That's the 50ms moment.
