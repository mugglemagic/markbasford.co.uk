# Chapter 07 Research — Gamification, Wins, and Why Small Success Matters

## Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan

### Core Framework
- Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (SDT): a broad framework for understanding human motivation, first developed in the 1970s–80s, formally published 2000.
- Three basic psychological needs that must be fulfilled for growth and wellbeing:
  1. **Autonomy** — the need to feel volitional and in control of one's own behaviour.
  2. **Competence** — the need to feel effective and capable.
  3. **Relatedness** — the need to feel connected to others.
- Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET), a sub-theory of SDT, specifically addresses intrinsic motivation — "motivation based on the satisfactions of behaving for its own sake."

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation
- Intrinsic motivation: doing something because it is inherently interesting or enjoyable.
- Extrinsic motivation: doing something because it leads to a separable outcome (reward, avoidance of punishment).
- Key finding: external rewards can *undermine* intrinsic motivation (the overjustification effect). When people are paid for something they'd do for free, they often enjoy it less.

## SDT and Gamification

### The Good
- Well-designed gamification supports all three basic needs: autonomy (meaningful choices), competence (progressive challenges and feedback), relatedness (social features and community).
- Progress indicators, skill trees, and milestone celebrations can genuinely support competence needs.
- Research confirms SDT plays a significant role in gamification practice because it covers the spectrum from extrinsic to intrinsic motivation.

### The Dangerous
- Badly designed gamification leans entirely on extrinsic rewards: points, badges, leaderboards (PBL). These can crowd out intrinsic motivation.
- If the gamification is removed, the behaviour often stops — because the person was never intrinsically motivated, just chasing the reward.
- The line between meaningful encouragement and manipulative loop: does the system support the user's autonomy, or does it exploit variable reinforcement to extract engagement?

## Operant Conditioning and Variable Ratio Schedules

### B.F. Skinner's Framework
- Operant conditioning: behaviour is shaped by its consequences (reinforcement or punishment).
- Reinforcement schedules determine how and when behaviour is rewarded.

### Variable Ratio Schedules
- Variable-ratio (VR) reinforcement: reward is delivered after an unpredictable number of responses.
- Most resistant to extinction — the unpredictability maintains engagement and prevents boredom.
- This is the mechanism behind slot machines, social media feeds, and loot boxes. Highly effective at maintaining high-frequency responses.
- The ethical question: VR schedules work, but they work by exploiting uncertainty. When a product uses VR reinforcement, is it encouraging the user or manipulating them?

## The Ethical Line

- The chapter's central tension: small wins genuinely matter for human wellbeing (SDT proves this). But the mechanisms that deliver small wins can also be weaponised.
- Duolingo's streak: supports competence (you're building a skill) and relatedness (friends see your streak). But the guilt mechanics around breaking a streak cross into manipulation territory.
- The test: if you removed the gamification, would the user still want to use the product? If yes, the gamification is enhancing something real. If no, it's a Skinner box.
