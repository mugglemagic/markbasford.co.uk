# Chapter 11 Research — The Present-Tense Interface

## Positive Psychology — Seligman's PERMA Model

### The Framework
- Martin Seligman (2011, *Flourish*): the PERMA model identifies five elements that contribute to human flourishing:
  1. **Positive emotions** — joy, gratitude, hope, contentment.
  2. **Engagement** — being fully absorbed in activities (flow).
  3. **Relationships** — meaningful connections with others.
  4. **Meaning** — belonging to and serving something larger than yourself.
  5. **Accomplishment** — pursuing mastery for its own sake.
- PERMA captures both hedonic wellbeing (feeling good) and eudaimonic wellbeing (living well).

### Research Foundation
- Seligman used his 1998 APA presidential address to shift psychology's focus from pathology to studying what makes life worth living.
- Meta-analysis of 49 studies: Positive Psychology Interventions (PPIs) produced improvements in wellbeing and lower depression. Interventions included writing gratitude letters, replaying positive experiences, and socialising.
- Momentary PERMA research (2023): wellbeing can be measured at the level of daily moments, not just life satisfaction. This supports the book's thesis — small moments are where wellbeing actually lives.

## Living in the Present Tense

### The About Time Connection
- The book's final chapter returns to the film's closing lesson: stop trying to go back and fix things. Live each day as if you chose it.
- Products that live in the present tense: they show you what's happening now, what you can do next, and how things are going — without dwelling on past errors or demanding future commitments.
- The present-tense interface doesn't guilt you about yesterday's inactivity or pressure you about tomorrow's deadline. It meets you where you are.

### Savouring Revisited
- Bryant's savouring strategies map to interface design:
  - **Sensory-perceptual sharpening** → thoughtful visual and haptic feedback that rewards attention.
  - **Temporal awareness** → "you've been working for 45 minutes — time for a break?" (acknowledging the present moment).
  - **Absorption** → flow-supporting interfaces that minimise interruption.
  - **Counting blessings** → progress summaries, "you've completed 3 of 5 tasks today."

## Eudaimonic Design

### Beyond Hedonic Pleasure
- Most product design optimises for hedonic outcomes: delight, satisfaction, reduced friction.
- Eudaimonic design asks: does this product help people live according to their values? Does it support their sense of meaning and purpose?
- A to-do app that helps you see what you accomplished (meaning, accomplishment) vs. one that only shows what's left (anxiety, inadequacy).
- A reading app that tracks pages read (competence, accomplishment) vs. one that tracks time spent (surveillance, guilt).

### Products as Daily Companions
- Most software is used daily: email, messaging, task management, calendars. These are daily companions.
- Daily companions shape the texture of a day. A companion that greets you, helps you recover from mistakes, celebrates small wins, and respects your time makes the day lighter.
- This is the book's closing argument: the ordinary interface is the extraordinary opportunity. Most products already occupy a slot in someone's daily life. The question is whether they treat that slot with care.

## The Closing Arc

### What the Book Resolves
- Chapter 01 posed the question via About Time: what if you could go back and experience any moment again? Would you?
- Chapter 11 answers: you don't need to go back. The moment is here, now, in the interface someone is using right this second. The question is whether the person who built it paid attention.
- The craft of frontend development — the microinteractions, the microcopy, the loading states, the error recovery — is the practice of noticing. Of caring about the moment someone is in, right now.

### The Final Quote
- Return to the About Time quote: "I just try to live every day as if I've deliberately come back to this one day."
- The invitation to the reader: build every interface as if you'd deliberately come back to use it. As if this interaction — this form, this loading screen, this error message — is the one you chose to return to.

## Story Candidates
1. The morning routine — the first app you open, the first notification you see, the first loading screen you wait through. That's the texture of your morning. Someone built that.
2. A letter from a friend vs. a marketing email — both arrive in the same inbox, but one was written with care and the other was optimised for conversion. Products can be either.
3. The About Time ending — Tim walking through his day, noticing everything, because he's decided this is the day he chose. The present-tense interface does the same thing for its users.
